My Power BI Learning Journey: From Zero to Dashboard

Published: December 15, 2024 | Category: Learning Journey

The Beginning: Why I Started

When I began my MSc in Information Systems at the University of West London, Power BI was just another tool mentioned in the curriculum. I had some experience with Excel, but business intelligence seemed like an intimidating field reserved for data scientists.

Everything changed when I started my role at CPRE. They needed someone to help make sense of their fundraising data, and suddenly I was thrown into the deep end of Power BI development.

The Learning Curve: Steeper Than Expected

My first attempt at creating a dashboard was... humbling. I spent three days trying to create what I now know is a simple bar chart. Here were my biggest early mistakes:

  • Trying to build everything at once: I wanted my first dashboard to do everything - track donations, segment donors, predict trends, and more. I learned to start simple.
  • Ignoring data quality: I assumed the data was clean and spent hours troubleshooting visualizations when the real issue was messy source data.
  • Overcomplicating DAX: I tried to write complex formulas before mastering basic functions like SUM() and AVERAGE().

The Breakthrough Moment

My breakthrough came when I stopped trying to impress and started focusing on solving actual business problems. Instead of building flashy visualizations, I asked my colleagues: "What questions do you need answered?"

This shift in mindset led to my first successful dashboard - a simple donation tracking report that showed daily totals, top donors, and campaign performance. It wasn't fancy, but it saved the team 2 hours of manual work every week.

Current Projects and What's Next

Today, I'm working on more complex integrations at CPRE, including automated data flows and advanced analytics. I'm also preparing for the PL-200 certification to expand my Power Platform skills.

But the most rewarding part isn't the technical complexity - it's seeing how these tools genuinely help people make better decisions and save time for more meaningful work.

Advice for Fellow Beginners

  1. Start with real problems: Don't build dashboards just for practice. Find actual questions that need answering.
  2. Embrace the basics: Master simple charts before attempting complex visualizations.
  3. Learn DAX gradually: Start with SUM(), COUNT(), and AVERAGE(). Everything else builds on these.
  4. Join the community: The Power BI community is incredibly welcoming and helpful.
  5. Document your learning: Keep notes on what works and what doesn't. You'll reference them later.

5 Power Automate Mistakes I Made (So You Don't Have To)

Published: December 10, 2024 | Category: Power Automate

When I started building automated workflows for CPRE's volunteer management system, I thought it would be straightforward. After all, Power Automate has a visual interface - how hard could it be?

Turns out, quite hard when you don't know what you're doing. Here are the five biggest mistakes I made, so you can learn from my experience rather than repeating it.

Mistake #1: Not Testing with Sample Data First

What I Did Wrong: I built my entire flow using live volunteer data from day one. When something went wrong (and it did), it affected real records.

The Consequence: I accidentally created 50 duplicate volunteer records before realizing my duplicate detection logic wasn't working correctly.

The Fix: Always start with a small sample dataset or test records. I now create 5-10 test records first and run my flows against those until everything works perfectly.

Mistake #2: Forgetting Error Handling

What I Did Wrong: My first flows had no error handling. When something failed (missing fields, API timeouts, invalid data), the entire process just stopped with no notification.

The Fix: Now I add error handling to every action that could fail. My standard approach includes timeout settings, "Configure run after" settings, email notifications, and error logging.

Mistake #3: Making Flows Too Complex Too Quickly

What I Did Wrong: I tried to automate the entire volunteer lifecycle in one massive flow.

The Fix: I broke it down into smaller, focused flows. Each flow does one thing well, making them easier to maintain and troubleshoot.

What I'd Do Differently Now

If I were starting the volunteer integration project today, my approach would be:

  1. Discovery phase: Spend more time understanding the current process
  2. Prototype quickly: Build a minimal working version first
  3. Test thoroughly: Use sample data and involve real users
  4. Deploy gradually: Start small and scale up
  5. Monitor closely: Watch for issues and gather feedback

Getting Started with Dynamics 365: A Student's Perspective

Published: December 5, 2024 | Category: Dynamics 365

As an MSc student working with Dynamics 365 at CPRE, I've discovered that CRM systems are far more powerful than I initially thought. Here's what I've learned and what I wish someone had told me when I started.

What I Thought CRM Was

Before working with Dynamics 365, I thought CRM was just a fancy contact database. Store names, emails, phone numbers - done. How wrong I was!

What CRM Actually Is

CRM is the central nervous system of an organization's relationship management. At CPRE, it tracks:

  • Donor interactions and giving history
  • Volunteer engagement and activities
  • Campaign performance and attribution
  • Communication preferences and compliance
  • Event participation and feedback

Key Lessons for Students

  1. Start with the business process: Understand how the organization works before diving into configuration
  2. Data quality is everything: Clean data in = useful insights out
  3. User adoption matters most: The best system is useless if people don't use it
  4. Integration is key: CRM works best when connected to other systems

Resources That Helped Me

  • Microsoft Learn modules for Dynamics 365
  • Hands-on practice in trial environments
  • YouTube tutorials for specific scenarios
  • Community forums for troubleshooting
